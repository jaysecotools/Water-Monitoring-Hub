Physical Parameters

Temperature (°C)

Measures the thermal energy in water. Affects metabolic rates of aquatic organisms and solubility of gases.

Freshwater Ideal: 18-22°C (varies by species)
Marine Ideal: 22-26°C (tropical), 14-18°C (temperate)
Danger Zone: >30°C or <10°C for most species
Horiba Tip: Allow 2 minutes for stabilization. Use the U-50's temperature compensation feature when measuring other parameters.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Rinse probe with sample water
  2. Submerge probe completely in water
  3. Wait for reading to stabilize (2-3 minutes)
  4. Record value when stable

Common Pitfalls

  • Taking measurements in direct sunlight
  • Not allowing enough stabilization time
  • Measuring near thermal discharges

Australian Case Study

During the 2016 Great Barrier Reef bleaching event, sustained water temperatures of 30-32°C caused widespread coral mortality. Regular monitoring helps identify thermal stress before damage occurs.

Turbidity (NTU)

Measures water clarity by detecting light scattering from suspended particles. Affects light penetration and photosynthesis.

Freshwater Ideal: <5 NTU
Acceptable: 5-20 NTU
Poor: >20 NTU (blocks sunlight)
Marine Coral Ideal: <1 NTU
Horiba Tip: Clean sensor window before measurement. Avoid air bubbles. For high turbidity (>100 NTU), use dilution method.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Clean sensor window with soft cloth
  2. Rinse with sample water
  3. Submerge sensor and avoid bubbles
  4. Take reading when stable (10-20 sec)
  5. For high turbidity, dilute sample 1:1 with DI water and multiply result by 2

Common Pitfalls

  • Scratched or dirty sensor window
  • Air bubbles on sensor surface
  • Not accounting for colored water (use transparency tube if needed)

Australian Case Study

In the Murray-Darling Basin, turbidity levels often spike after heavy rains due to soil erosion. Monitoring helps track sediment loads that can smother aquatic habitats.

Conductivity (µS/cm or mS/cm)

Measures water's ability to conduct electricity, indicating dissolved ion concentration. Used to estimate salinity and detect pollution.

Freshwater Ideal: 50-1500 µS/cm (varies by region)
Freshwater Poor: >2500 µS/cm (salinity stress)
Marine Normal: ~50 mS/cm (varies with salinity)
Horiba Tip: Rinse probe between measurements. Use the appropriate range setting (µS/cm for freshwater, mS/cm for marine).

Measurement Protocol

  1. Select appropriate range (µS/cm or mS/cm)
  2. Rinse probe with DI water
  3. Submerge probe completely
  4. Wait for reading to stabilize (30-60 sec)
  5. Record value when stable

Common Pitfalls

  • Not rinsing between samples (cross-contamination)
  • Using wrong range setting
  • Measuring near bottom sediments

Australian Case Study

In Western Australia's wheatbelt regions, rising groundwater has brought salts to the surface, increasing conductivity in freshwater systems. Regular monitoring helps track these changes.

Salinity (ppt)

Measures total dissolved salts in water. Critical for osmoregulation in marine organisms.

Open Ocean: 33-37 ppt
Estuaries: 0-30 ppt (varies with tide)
Stressful: <20 ppt or >40 ppt for most marine species
Horiba Tip: Calibrate with standard seawater (35 ppt) monthly. Rinse thoroughly after use in high salinity.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Calibrate with standard solution if needed
  2. Rinse probe with DI water
  3. Submerge probe completely
  4. Wait for reading to stabilize (1-2 minutes)
  5. Record value when stable

Common Pitfalls

  • Not accounting for temperature effects
  • Salt buildup on probe
  • Measuring in stratified water columns

Australian Case Study

In the Coorong estuary (SA), drought conditions have caused hypersalinity (>100 ppt) events, drastically changing the ecosystem. Monitoring helps manage water releases.

Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) (mg/L)

Measures all inorganic and organic substances dissolved in water. Correlates with conductivity.

Freshwater Ideal: <500 mg/L
Acceptable: 500-1000 mg/L
Poor: >1000 mg/L
Marine Normal: ~35,000 mg/L
Horiba Tip: Most Horiba meters calculate TDS from conductivity. For accurate results, set the appropriate conversion factor (usually 0.5-0.7).

Measurement Protocol

  1. Set correct conversion factor in meter settings
  2. Measure conductivity first
  3. Allow meter to calculate TDS
  4. Record both values

Common Pitfalls

  • Using wrong conversion factor
  • Not distinguishing between TDS and suspended solids
  • Organic compounds not detected by conductivity

Seawater Specific Gravity (unitless)

Ratio of seawater density to pure water density. Used in marine aquaria and aquaculture.

Natural Seawater: 1.023-1.028
Coral Reefs: 1.025-1.027
Stressful: <1.020 or >1.030
Horiba Tip: Specific gravity is temperature-dependent. Use temperature compensation or measure at standard temperature (usually 25°C).

Measurement Protocol

  1. Calibrate refractometer if used
  2. Ensure sample is at standard temperature
  3. Take reading and record temperature
  4. Apply temperature correction if needed

Common Pitfalls

  • Not temperature compensating
  • Bubbles in refractometer sample
  • Using expired calibration standards

Chemical Parameters

pH Level

Measures acidity/alkalinity on a 0-14 scale. Affects chemical processes and organism health.

Freshwater Ideal: 6.5-8.0
Marine Ideal: 7.8-8.4
Danger Zone: <6.0 or >9.0
Horiba Tip: Calibrate with pH 4, 7, and 10 buffers monthly. Rinse electrode with DI water between measurements.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Calibrate meter with fresh buffers
  2. Rinse electrode with DI water
  3. Submerge electrode in sample
  4. Wait for reading to stabilize (1-2 min)
  5. Record value when stable
  6. Rinse and store with storage solution

Common Pitfalls

  • Using expired buffer solutions
  • Dirty or dried out electrode
  • Not temperature compensating
  • Measuring in low ionic strength water

Australian Case Study

Acid sulfate soils in coastal NSW can release sulfuric acid when drained, causing pH crashes below 4.0 that kill aquatic life. Monitoring helps identify at-risk areas.

Dissolved Oxygen (mg/L)

Oxygen available for aquatic respiration. Critical for fish and invertebrate survival.

Ideal: >6 mg/L
Stressful: 4-6 mg/L
Lethal: <2 mg/L
% Saturation Ideal: 80-120%
Horiba Tip: Replace DO membrane every 1-2 months. Stir gently during measurement for accurate readings. Store with storage solution.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Check membrane condition
  2. Calibrate to 100% in water-saturated air
  3. Submerge probe in flowing water
  4. Stir gently until reading stabilizes (1-2 min)
  5. Record both mg/L and % saturation

Common Pitfalls

  • Damaged or dry membrane
  • Not stirring during measurement
  • Measuring in stagnant water
  • Not temperature compensating

Australian Case Study

In the Hawkesbury-Nepean River, summer algal blooms can cause nighttime oxygen crashes below 2 mg/L, resulting in fish kills. Continuous DO monitoring helps predict these events.

Oxidation-Reduction Potential (ORP) (mV)

Measures water's ability to oxidize or reduce substances. Indicates chemical and biological activity.

Aerobic: +100 to +300 mV
Transitional: 0 to +100 mV
Anaerobic: <0 mV
Horiba Tip: Calibrate with ORP standard solution (usually +250 mV) monthly. Rinse probe thoroughly between measurements.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Calibrate with ORP standard
  2. Rinse probe with DI water
  3. Submerge probe in sample
  4. Wait for reading to stabilize (2-3 min)
  5. Record stable value

Common Pitfalls

  • Dirty or contaminated probe
  • Not allowing enough stabilization time
  • Measuring in low conductivity water

Ammonia (NH₃) (mg/L)

Toxic nitrogen compound from waste and decomposition. More toxic at higher pH and temperature.

Safe: <0.1 mg/L
Alert: 0.1-0.5 mg/L
Toxic: >0.5 mg/L
Horiba Tip: Use the ammonia ISE probe for direct measurement. For colorimetric tests, ensure proper reaction time and avoid air bubbles.

Measurement Protocol

  1. For ISE: calibrate with standards
  2. For colorimetric: add reagents precisely
  3. Wait exact reaction time
  4. Measure in clean cuvette
  5. Record value

Common Pitfalls

  • Not accounting for pH/temperature effects
  • Expired reagents
  • Incorrect reaction times
  • Interference from other nitrogen compounds

Australian Case Study

In aquaculture ponds, ammonia spikes often occur after overfeeding. The Northern Territory Barramundi farms use continuous monitoring to prevent losses during warm months when toxicity increases.

Nitrate (NO₃⁻) (mg/L)

Oxidized nitrogen form from ammonia breakdown. Causes eutrophication at high levels.

Natural: <0.5 mg/L
Elevated: 0.5-5 mg/L
Eutrophic: >5 mg/L
Horiba Tip: Use the nitrate ISE for direct measurement. For colorimetric tests, ensure no air bubbles in cuvette and proper zeroing.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Calibrate with nitrate standards
  2. For colorimetric: add reagents in order
  3. Wait specified reaction time
  4. Measure absorbance at correct wavelength
  5. Record concentration

Common Pitfalls

  • Interference from chloride ions
  • Not filtering turbid samples
  • Using wrong wavelength

Australian Case Study

In the Great Barrier Reef catchment, sugarcane farming contributes to nitrate levels >10 mg/L in runoff. Monitoring helps target improved farming practices to reduce reef impacts.

Nitrite (NO₂⁻) (mg/L)

Intermediate in nitrogen cycle. Highly toxic to fish even at low concentrations.

Safe: <0.1 mg/L
Alert: 0.1-0.5 mg/L
Toxic: >0.5 mg/L
Horiba Tip: Nitrite measurements are sensitive to interference. Use fresh reagents and measure promptly after development.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Use fresh reagents
  2. Follow exact reaction times
  3. Measure absorbance immediately
  4. Compare to standard curve

Common Pitfalls

  • Interference from organic matter
  • Not measuring immediately after development
  • Using expired reagents

Phosphate (PO₄³⁻) (mg/L)

Key nutrient limiting plant growth. Excess causes algal blooms and eutrophication.

Oligotrophic: <0.03 mg/L
Mesotrophic: 0.03-0.1 mg/L
Eutrophic: >0.1 mg/L
Horiba Tip: For low-level phosphate measurements, use the ascorbic acid method and ensure all glassware is acid-washed.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Filter sample if turbid
  2. Add reagents in specified order
  3. Wait exact reaction time
  4. Measure absorbance at 880 nm
  5. Compare to standard curve

Common Pitfalls

  • Phosphate contamination from detergents
  • Not filtering samples
  • Incorrect reaction times

Australian Case Study

In Sydney's Georges River, stormwater inputs have created phosphate hotspots >0.5 mg/L, triggering cyanobacteria blooms. Monitoring guides remediation efforts.

Biological Parameters

Chlorophyll-a (µg/L)

Primary photosynthetic pigment in algae. Indicator of algal biomass and productivity.

Low Nutrient: <10 µg/L
Moderate: 10-30 µg/L
High Nutrient: >30 µg/L
Horiba Tip: For in vivo measurement, avoid direct sunlight. For extraction methods, use fresh filters and keep samples dark.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Filter known water volume
  2. Extract with acetone (90%)
  3. Centrifuge if needed
  4. Measure fluorescence/absorbance
  5. Calculate from standard curve

Common Pitfalls

  • Pigment degradation in light
  • Incomplete extraction
  • Interference from pheophytin

Australian Case Study

In the Swan-Canning Estuary (WA), chlorophyll-a levels >50 µg/L indicate harmful algal blooms. Continuous fluorometers provide early warning to water managers.

Standard Measurement Protocols

Horiba U-50/U-52 Field Measurement Procedure

Pre-Field Preparation

  1. Charge battery fully
  2. Check sensor membranes (DO, pH)
  3. Prepare calibration standards
  4. Pack cleaning supplies (DI water, tissues)
  5. Check all probes are secure

On-Site Procedure

  1. Power on meter and allow to stabilize
  2. Rinse all probes with site water
  3. Perform calibrations if needed (pH, DO)
  4. Submerge probes in flowing water
  5. Wait for readings to stabilize
  6. Record all parameters
  7. Rinse probes before next site

Post-Field Care

  1. Rinse all probes with DI water
  2. Store pH electrode in storage solution
  3. Keep DO membrane moist
  4. Download data promptly
  5. Recharge battery

Common Field Mistakes

  • Not allowing enough stabilization time
  • Measuring in stagnant water
  • Dirty or damaged probes
  • Not recording site conditions
  • Improper probe storage
